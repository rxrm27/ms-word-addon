# WIPO Patent Drafting Manual — Claim Rules Reference
## Source: WIPO Patent Drafting Manual (2nd ed., 2023), Pages 42–71
## Module IV: Fundamentals of Claim Drafting (sections 2 & 3) + Module V intro

---

## 1. PATENT CLAIM FORMAT (pp. 42–53)

### General Rule
A patent claim is written as a **single sentence** in the **present tense**. Each claim begins on a new line and is numbered. The claims section typically appears toward the end of a patent application.

---

### 2.1 Parts of a Claim (pp. 43–46)

A patent claim has **three parts**: Preamble, Transitional Phrase, and Body.

#### Preamble
- Introductory phrase identifying the **category** of invention (apparatus, article, composition, compound, device, system, method, process)
- Written as a **singular noun phrase** (may include premodifiers/postmodifiers)
- More adjectives/complements = narrower preamble
- Should be at the **right level of breadth** — not inadvertently limit scope
- May or may not be accorded patentable weight depending on jurisdiction
- Rule: Preamble must "breathe life" into the claim as a whole to be accorded weight (US practice)

**Examples:**
- Broad: "An apparatus for cooking,"
- Medium: "An apparatus for cooking grains,"
- Narrow: "An apparatus for cooking rice,"

#### Transitional Phrase
Two types:

**Open-ended (inclusive):**
- "comprising" — includes listed elements AND allows additional elements (most common in US)
- "including" — same effect as comprising
- "containing" — same effect
- "characterized by" — same effect (used in some jurisdictions)
- Interpretation: "including the following elements but not excluding others"

**Closed (exclusive):**
- "consisting of" — limits claim to ONLY the recited elements; nothing more
- Warning: infringers can avoid by adding one more element
- Use only when simplification over prior art is the point
- Percentages in "consisting of" chemical claims must sum to 100%

**Semi-closed:**
- "consisting essentially of" — allows only elements that do not materially affect basic and novel properties (used in some jurisdictions)

#### Body
- Recites and interrelates all elements of the claim
- May include structural, functional, relational, parametric, activity elements
- Claims cannot be a **mere list of parts** — elements must be **connected** to each other
- Open-ended and closed phrases can also appear in the body to define sub-elements

**Structural model:**
```
Preamble, transitional phrase:
  • Element #1;
  • Element #2; and
  • Element #3.
```

**Rejected form (unconnected list):**
```
1. An apparatus for holding items, comprising:
  • four legs;
  • 16 screws; and
  • a top.
```

**Allowed form (connected):**
```
1. An apparatus for holding items, comprising:
  • at least one leg; and
  • a top configured to be supported by at least one leg.
```

---

### 2.2 Grammar of a Claim (pp. 46–49)

#### Punctuation
- **Comma** separates preamble from transitional phrase
- **Colon** separates transitional phrase from body
- Body elements separated by **semicolons**, last element ends with **period**
- Elements listed with bullet points or line indentation (required in US)

#### Antecedent Basis
- First mention of element: use **indefinite article** "a" or "an"
- Subsequent mentions of same element: use **definite article** "the" (or "said" — archaic)
- Violation = §112(b) / Art. 84 EPC defect (indefiniteness/lack of clarity)
- In each **new claim set**, antecedent basis must be re-established from scratch
- To distinguish two instances of same element: use "first" / "second" or "another"

**Correct example:**
```
1. A device, comprising:
  • a pencil; and
  • a light attached to the pencil.
2. The device recited in claim 1 wherein the light is detachably attached to the pencil.
```

#### Reference Numerals
- PCT practice: reference numerals in parentheses after elements, e.g., "the memory (123)"
- US practice: generally avoided or prohibited (risk of limiting interpretation)
- Numerals do not incorporate drawings into claims
- Additional text inside parentheses (beyond bare numbers) may be treated as limiting

#### Connective Words (Wherein, Whereby, Such that)
- "Wherein" — describes a function, operation or result that flows from prior structure
- "Whereby" — similar connective function
- Must be used where the result **necessarily follows** from the recited structure

#### Multiple Elements
- Most patent offices require at least **two elements** in a claim
- Single-element claims are typically impossibly broad and will be rejected

---

### 2.3 Two-Part Claims / Improvement Claims (pp. 49–51)

Also called: Jepson claim (US), improvement claim

**Structure:**
- Part 1 (Preamble extended): states the single most relevant **known prior art**
- Transition: "characterized by" or "characterized in that" (EPO/Europe), "wherein the improvement comprises" (US)
- Part 2 (Body): recites the **novel/inventive element(s)**

**Key rule:** Using two-part format = **explicit admission** that Part 1 subject matter was in the prior art at filing date.

**EPO preference:** Two-part format preferred where appropriate. Exceptions:
- Combination of known integers of equal status (inventive step lies solely in combination)
- Modification of a known chemical process (omitting/substituting one substance)
- Complex functionally interrelated system with changes in several parts/relationships
- New chemical compound or group of compounds

---

### 2.4 Alternative Elements and Markush Claims (pp. 51–53)

**Issue with "or":** In patent claims, "metal or plastic" is generally too unclear.

**Accepted alternatives:**
- "metal and/or plastic" (inclusive or)
- "at least one composition selected from metal and plastic" (inclusive)
- "either metal or plastic" (exclusive or)

**Markush Claim Format:**
> "wherein element A is selected from the group consisting of X, Y and Z"

**Rules:**
- Default: **one member at a time** unless "and combinations thereof" is added
- Example: "wherein the solvent is selected from the group consisting of A, B, C and combinations thereof"
- Must not be ambiguous
- Common in chemistry; use generic terms (e.g., "a fastener") or functional language in other fields
- **Critical risk:** If one alternative in a Markush group is found in prior art, the **entire claim lacks novelty**
- Must cover all patentable embodiments — don't omit valid alternatives
- Must meet unity of invention requirement

---

### 2.5 Functional Elements and Means-Plus-Function Claims (pp. 53–54)

**Functional language ways:**
1. Define element by its function: "a computer-readable storage medium," "an oxidizing agent"
2. Generic structural word implying function: "a heater," "a fastener," "a solvent"
3. Functional phrase after element: "providing [operation]," "creating [property]," "configured to [result]," "such that [achieved]," "whereby [effect]," "so that [end]"

**Means-plus-function (M+F) format:**
- "means for [performing function]"
- Interpreted as: disclosed structure in spec + reasonable equivalents
- Can be interpreted both broadly AND narrowly — scope depends on spec

**M+F rules:**
- Must have adequate structural description in specification
- Avoid reciting unnecessary structures that narrow interpretation
- Avoid failing to clearly identify which structures perform the function
- Mixing M+F and non-M+F claims in same patent is allowed
- Risk: if ALL elements defined functionally → may be rejected as "mere desideratum" (desired result without how)

## 2. CLAIM SETS (pp. 54–71)

### General Rules for Claim Sets (p. 54–55)

- Patent specifications **typically contain more than one claim**
- Balance: scope of protection vs. cost vs. legal clarity
- **Cost factors:** excess claim fees; examination fees; grant fees vary by count
- **Quality factors:** too broad → rejected for novelty/inventive step; too narrow → easily designed around
- **Clarity factor:** proliferation of dependent claims can complicate; number should reflect invention's complexity

---

### 3.1 Independent Claims (pp. 55–56)

**Definition:** Stands alone; does not depend on another claim for any limitation.

**Rules:**
- Always **broadest** in its dependency chain
- Each claim set begins with an independent claim
- May have **multiple independent claims** in one application (covering different aspects)
- Independent claims may cover: different aspects, the same aspect at different breadths, different claim types (apparatus + method)
- **Unity of invention:** All independent claims must relate to one invention or a group of inventions forming a single general inventive concept (PCT Rule 13; EPC Rule 44; US 35 USC §121)
- Patent offices can require divisional applications if unity is violated

---

### 3.2 Dependent Claims (pp. 56–61)

**Definition:** Refers back to and further **limits** another claim (independent or dependent).

**Dependency reference format:**
- "The device according to claim 1, wherein..."
- "The device of claim 1, further comprising..."
- "The method of claim 1, wherein the step of X further comprises..."

**Rules:**
- Every dependent claim must add **at least one new limitation** not present in the referenced claim
- If a dependent claim merely restates the parent claim without adding anything, it is redundant and will be rejected
- A dependent claim's scope is always **narrower** than the claim it references
- Any claim (independent or dependent) may serve as the base for another dependent claim

**Purposes of dependent claims:**
- Cover preferred/commercial embodiments
- Create **fallback positions** if independent claim is found not novel or obvious
- Narrow to specific implementations (easier to prove infringement)
- Cover alternative embodiments
- Address jurisdiction-specific eligibility requirements

**Ordering (best practice):**
- Draft dependent claims in order of decreasing breadth
- Start with claims adding single important limitations; progress to very specific embodiments

---

### 3.3 Multiple Dependent Claims (pp. 61–64)

**Definition:** A claim that refers back to **more than one previous claim** in the alternative.

**Format:**
> "The composition according to any one of Claims 1–3, wherein..."
> "The apparatus of Claims 1 or 2, further comprising..."

**Jurisdiction rules:**
- **Allowed:** EPO (including multiple-multiple), Japan, most PCT jurisdictions
- **Not allowed:** United States, Republic of Korea — multiple-multiple dependent claims prohibited
- **US note:** USPTO charges extra fee per multiple dependent claim (whether numbered or not)

**Multiple-multiple dependent claims (EPO/Japan only):**
A multiple dependent claim used as base for another multiple dependent claim:
```
Claim 4. The apparatus of any one of Claims 1–3, wherein the light is an LED light.
```
- US/Korea: Claim 4 above is not permissible

**"Any one of" vs. "either...or":**
- "Any one of" = inclusive alternative (any single one)
- "Either...or" = exclusive (one or the other, not both — use carefully)

**Key benefit of multiple dependent claims:**
- Fewer total claims needed to cover all combinations
- Enables **amendment fallback** — if prior art destroys independent claim, can select specific combination from multiple dependents
- Critical for amendment strategy during prosecution and nullity/opposition

**Example of value:**
Without multiple dependencies: 6 claims cannot protect specific combination A1+B3.
With multiple dependencies: same 6 claims CAN protect A1+B3 via Claim 6 depending from Claim 2.

---

### 3.4 Claims Referring to Features of Another Claim (pp. 64–66)

An **independent claim** may incorporate features of another claim (EPO practice):

**Format examples:**
```
2. A data-processing apparatus comprising means for carrying out the method as defined in Claim 1.
3. A computer program product comprising instructions which, when the program is executed by a computer, cause the computer to carry out the method as defined in Claim 1.
```

**Pharmaceutical multi-claim example:**
- Claim 1: Compound of formula
- Claim 5: Pharmaceutical composition comprising compound as defined in any one of Claims 1–4
- Claim 6: Compound as defined in any one of Claims 1–4 for use in medicine
- Claim 7: Use of compound as defined in any one of Claims 1–4 for manufacture of medicament

**Benefit:** Significantly reduces total claim count while achieving full coverage.

**EPO rule:** If claimed process results in a product defined by a patentable product claim, no separate novelty/inventive step examination required for process claim (provided all product features inevitably result from the process).

---

### 3.5 Claim Sets Based on Set Theory (pp. 66–71)

**Visual model:** Claim scopes form nested sets.
- Independent claim = outermost set A (broadest)
- Dependent claims = subsets within A
- Wider set = broader claim; subset = narrower claim

**Infringement logic (All-Elements Rule):**
1. Infringement of narrower dependent claim → necessarily infringes the broader independent claim it depends on
2. **Infringement of independent claim ≠ infringement of all dependent claims** (common mistake)
3. If accused embodiment falls outside independent claim scope → no claim in that dependency chain is infringed
4. If accused embodiment falls within a dependent claim → infringes that dependent claim AND all claims in the chain up to independent

**Practical example:**
- Accused product Q1 outside Claim 1 → none of Claims 1–5 infringed
- Accused product Q2 inside Claim 3 → Claims 1, 2, 3 infringed; Claims 4, 5 NOT infringed

---

### 3.6 Schematic Example of Drafting a Claim Set (pp. 71–76)

**Drafting process — ordered steps:**

1. **Brainstorming with inventor:**
   - Identify the invention(s)
   - Select claim type for first independent claim
   - Choose preamble at appropriate breadth
   - Account for: specific embodiments, drawings, closest prior art

2. **Draft Independent Claim:**
   - State essential elements achieving the technical effect
   - Stretch elements to greatest reasonable extent consistent with essential functionality and prior art
   - Do NOT claim "mere desiderata" (result without mechanism)
   - Every element must appear in specification

3. **Formulate inventive step argument for each claim:**
   - Identify what is nonobvious / produces unexpected advantage
   - Product claim: define elements giving rise to advantage, not the advantage itself

4. **Draft Dependent Claims:**
   - Cover more particular/specific embodiments
   - Must be novel (automatically, if independent claim is novel)
   - Include "fallback positions" for prosecution/litigation
   - Identify order of importance of invention elements (essential vs. nonessential)
   - Consider commercial/business setting and enforcement context

## 3. TYPES OF ELEMENTS IN CLAIMS (p. 42)

| Element Type | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Structural | Defined by what they ARE | "a screw," "a DVD," "hydrogen peroxide" |
| Functional | Defined by what they DO | "a fastening means," "an oxidizing agent" |
| Relational | Relationships between elements | "attached," "electrically connected," "dissolved in" |
| Intentional | Purpose, introduced with "for" | "for coagulation," "for treating cancer" |
| Parametric | Measurable property values | flexural strength, resistance, melting point |
| Activity | Steps in process claims (gerunds) | "fixing," "reading," "reacting" |

## 4. KEY CLAIM VALIDITY RULES (cross-cutting)

| Rule | Description |
|---|---|
| All-elements rule | ALL elements of a claim must be present in accused embodiment for infringement |
| Antecedent basis | "a/an" on first use; "the/said" on subsequent use — per claim |
| Support requirement | Every claim element must have basis in description |
| Enablement | POSITA must be able to make/use invention from disclosure alone |
| Clarity/Definiteness | Claims must be clear and concise; no vague/ambiguous terms |
| No mere desideratum | Cannot claim desired result without saying how it is achieved |
| Unity of invention | All claims must link to a single general inventive concept |
| No added matter | Amendments cannot add subject matter beyond original filing |

## 5. JURISDICTION-SPECIFIC FORMAT NOTES

| Aspect | US (USPTO) | Europe (EPO) | PCT | Japan/Korea |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Transitional phrase "comprising" | Standard | Standard | Standard | Standard |
| Two-part claim | Optional ("Jepson") | Preferred | Not required | Optional |
| Reference numerals in claims | Generally avoided | Encouraged | Preferred (in parentheses) | Used |
| Multiple dependent claims | Allowed (extra fee) | Allowed | Allowed | Allowed |
| Multiple-multiple dependent claims | NOT allowed | Allowed | Varies | Korea: NOT allowed; Japan: Allowed |
| "Said" vs "the" | Both accepted | "the" preferred | "the" preferred | — |
| Claim location | After description (or "What is claimed is:") | Separate section | End of document | Before description (JPO/KIPO) |
